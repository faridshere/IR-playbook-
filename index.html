Incident Response Lifecycle

Nine phases that span from preventive preparation through post-incident learning. Each phase has clear responsibilities and deliverables.

PHASE 00 Pre-Incident

Preparation

Incident Response does not start with an alert â€” it starts before that. Weak preparation guarantees chaotic response regardless of the framework used.

  • Defined IR policy and escalation paths
  • Clear role assignments (SOC L1 / L2, IR Lead, IT, Management)
  • Secure, out-of-band communication channels
  • Full logging visibility: SIEM, EDR, identity logs, network logs
  • Ticketing system for incident tracking
  • Tabletop exercises and alert-quality reviews

PHASE 01 Detection

Alert & Initial Detection

A signal appears â€” SIEM alert, EDR detection, or a user helpdesk call. The critical first step is immediate record creation.

"Even if it later turns out to be harmless, documentation must start from the very beginning."
  • Open a ticket immediately upon signal receipt
  • SOC L1 performs first review: real or false positive?
  • Identify affected system(s) and whether activity is ongoing
  • Assess whether activity appears isolated or wider spread
  • Declare incident status â€” triggering the structured process

PHASE 02 Critical

The First Hour â€” Structure Under Pressure

The first hour is where most mistakes happen. Calm, structured action prevents compounding errors.

  • Assign an Incident Commander (usually IR Lead or L3)
  • Confirm communication channel â€” avoid compromised systems
  • Define initial severity level
  • Agree on first objective: stop spread / protect data / preserve evidence / stabilize service
  • Log every action with timestamp and owner

PHASE 03 Analysis

Triage & Impact Assessment

SOC L2 investigates deeply, focusing on understanding scope before diving into technical details.

  • How and when did this start?
  • Which systems and accounts are involved?
  • Is there evidence of lateral movement?
  • What is the business impact?
  • Prioritize based on data sensitivity, spread potential, and business criticality

PHASE 04 Scope

Scoping Before Deep Forensics

Resist the urge to dive deep into one machine. A broad-first approach prevents tunnel vision and wasted effort.

  • Search for indicators across the entire environment first
  • Review authentication logs for anomalies
  • Look for suspicious patterns at scale
  • Only after understanding the full picture â€” proceed to deep forensics
  • For legal / regulated breach scenarios: formal evidence preservation

PHASE 05 Containment

Containment â€” Stop the Damage

Containment must be fast but controlled. Actions can disrupt operations â€” management approval may be needed for critical systems.

  • Isolate affected endpoints from the network
  • Disable compromised accounts; revoke active sessions
  • Block malicious domains, IPs, or file hashes
  • Temporarily segment affected network segments
  • Preserve evidence â€” do not destroy artifacts accidentally
  • Two levels: immediate stop-spread actions + longer-term stabilization

PHASE 06 Eradication

Eradication â€” Remove the Root Cause

Once stable, remove every trace of attacker presence. Partial cleaning is not acceptable when full system integrity is uncertain.

  • Delete persistence mechanisms (scheduled tasks, registry keys, services)
  • Remove malicious accounts and credentials
  • Reset all affected credentials
  • Patch exploited vulnerabilities
  • Rebuild systems from clean backup when trust cannot be fully restored

PHASE 07 Recovery

Recovery â€” Return to Normal Safely

Recovery is gradual and supervised. Systems are not simply reconnected â€” they are brought back online in a controlled order.

  • Bring systems online in a prioritized, controlled sequence
  • Increase monitoring intensity during the recovery window
  • Add newly discovered indicators to detection tools
  • Validate restored systems before returning to production
  • Confirm business operations are stable before closing the incident

PHASE 08 Post-Incident

Lessons Learned â€” Turn Events Into Maturity

Often rushed but critically important. This phase turns a negative event into organizational capability. A final incident report is mandatory.

  • Root cause analysis: what actually happened?
  • What worked well? What slowed us down?
  • Were we missing access, tools, contacts, or clarity?
  • Final report: timeline, actions, impact, improvement plan
  • Update detection rules and refine playbooks
  • Recommend awareness training if human error contributed

The most balanced and operational lifecycle framework. Covers preparation, detection, containment, eradication, recovery, and post-incident review. Aligns naturally with SOC operations and documentation requirements. Forces completeness.
